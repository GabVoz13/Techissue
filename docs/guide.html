Sustainable Technology in a High-Energy World

Not all “green” tech is created equal. This guide looks at streaming, AI, and digital policy through a sustainability lens, drawing on research from my Zotero library.

Critical look at “green” tech

What This Guide Is Doing

“Digital” does not mean immaterial. Behind every video stream, AI model, and cloud service are data centres, networks, water, and hardware that eventually becomes e-waste.

This guide is built from my curated Zotero library on sustainable technology. It highlights how everyday habits connect to large infrastructures, and how design choices and policy decisions shape what counts as “green.”

How to read this

  • Start with the big picture of why sustainable tech matters.
  • Look at streaming as a concrete everyday case.
  • Use Green AI and Policy to think about system-level change.

Why sustainable technology matters Foundation

Even “virtual” actions rely on physical systems. Data centres draw on regional power grids and large volumes of cooling water. Networks span oceans and cities with fibre, towers, and routers. Every phone, laptop, and console begins in a mine and ends as part of a growing e-waste stream.

Sustainability research stresses that the environmental impact of digital technologies spans the entire lifecycle: from extraction and manufacturing to transport, usage, and disposal. Focusing only on how efficient a device is while it’s turned on hides the systems that make that efficiency possible.

Streaming & everyday media habits Case study

Streaming feels weightless, but every second depends on data moving between storage, networks, and devices. Analyses of video streaming, such as those by the Carbon Trust and related work in my Zotero library, show that higher resolutions and longer watch times increase energy use along the whole chain.

Some practical questions to ask:

  • Resolution vs. impact: Do I always need 4K, or is HD enough for most viewing?
  • Streaming vs. download: Would downloading once be less intensive than streaming the same thing repeatedly?
  • Transparency vs. green branding: Does a platform publish real environmental data, or just use vague eco language?

Green AI: potential and problems Systems in tension

Green AI is a term used for approaches that try to reduce the energy and resource demands of machine learning systems while keeping them useful. Studies in my Zotero collection track how model size, training time, and hardware choices affect total energy use and emissions.

Common strategies include:

  • Efficiency techniques: model pruning, quantization, and distillation to make models smaller and faster.
  • Hardware choices: using GPUs/TPUs and data centre infrastructure designed for high efficiency.
  • Accounting: actually measuring and reporting the energy and carbon cost of training and deployment.

At the same time, very large, general-purpose models can still require huge training runs and constant serving infrastructure. Later revisions of this guide will use specific Zotero sources to compare “smaller, task-specific” models with “bigger, general” ones in terms of both usefulness and sustainability.

Policy, governance, and responsibility Beyond individual choices

It is easy to focus only on individual actions—lowering resolutions, buying efficient devices, or unplugging hardware. Those choices matter, but they are layered on top of policies and infrastructures that ordinary users don’t directly control.

Policy and governance shape:

  • Device lifetimes: whether hardware is repairable and how quickly it is pushed out of support.
  • Reporting rules: what companies have to disclose about data centre emissions, water use, and supply chains.
  • Public infrastructure: investment in efficient grids, cooling, and network backbones.

Future updates to this guide will connect specific policies and regulatory frameworks from my Zotero readings to the technical issues discussed in earlier sections, reinforcing that sustainable technology is always a shared responsibility.
